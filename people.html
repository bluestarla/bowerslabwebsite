The Alfred W. Bowers
Laboratory of Anthropology

People

The Alfred W. Bowers Laboratory of Anthropology employees a Director and a Collections Manager to oversee the endowed and stored archaeological collections, develop, oversee, and lead public outreach initiatives, and seek out funding in the form of contracts with collection owners and grants with a variety of private, state, and federal agencies to sustain the lab's work.

In addition, the Bowers Laboratory plays an important role in training future archaeologists in archaeological curation, analysis, and collections management as well project management, marketing, social media, and public outreach. Every year we welcome approximately 6 to 10 students to work in our lab.

We also have a number of student interns from national universities to local high schools. If you are interested in becoming an intern, please email either Dr. Leah Evans-Janke or Dr. Stacey L. Camp.

We would love to hear from alumni and former employees/interns who worked in the lab. If you are willing, we would like to post updates about what you are doing now and memories of the lab.


Laboratory Director

DR. STACEY L. CAMP is an Associate Professor of Anthropology and the Director of the Alfred W. Bowers Laboratory of Anthropology at the University of Idaho. She completed her B.A. in both Anthropology and English & Comparative Studies at Occidental College in 2001, and finished her Ph.D. in Anthropology at Stanford University in 2009 under the supervision of her doctoral advisor, Dr. Barbara Voss, and committee members Dr. Ian Hodder, Dr. Paulla Ebron, and Dr. Lynn Meskell. She got her first taste of historical archaeology as a field school student of Dr. Charles E. Orser, Jr.'s archaeological project in Strokestown, Ireland in 1999, and has continued to develop her knowledge in the field since then. Her research involves the archaeology of immigration in late 19th and early 20th century in the American West, cultural heritage and memory, digital project management, pedagogy, and sustainability, and the integration of technology into archaeological student training and courses.
